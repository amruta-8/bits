Part 1: How to Approach Each Question Type

1. Identify-the-Type Questions (Data types, variables)

Strategy: Ask two questions in order:
  1. "Can I do maths on it?" No → Categorical (nominal/ordinal). Yes → Numerical.
  2. "Can I count it or must I measure it?" Count → Discrete. Measure → Continuous.
Common trap: Phone numbers, zip codes, jersey numbers look numerical but are nominal — you can't average them meaningfully.

2. Central Tendency & Spread Questions

Strategy:
  1. Always sort first. Many errors come from unsorted data.
  2. Compute mean, then median. If mean ≠ median, the data is skewed (mean chases the tail).
  3. For SD: use the shortcut $s^2 = \frac{\sum x_i^2 - n\bar{x}^2}{n-1}$ to avoid computing each $(x_i - \bar{x})^2$ separately.
  4. For IQR: split the sorted data in half, find median of each half.
  5. "At least one outlier" questions: compute fences $Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ and $Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$, check every value.

3. Skewness Questions

Quick Rule: Compare mean and mode (or mean and median):
  • Mean = Mode → Symmetric
  • Mean > Mode → Positive (right) skew (tail stretches right)
  • Mean < Mode → Negative (left) skew (tail stretches left)
Remember: the mean always leans toward the tail.

4. Probability Questions (Addition rule, counting)

Strategy:
  1. "At least one" → Use complement: $P(\text{at least 1}) = 1 - P(\text{none})$
  2. "A or B" → Addition rule: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$
  3. "A and B" with independence → Multiply: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$
  4. Counting (committees, selections): use $\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$. Favourable over total.
  5. With vs without replacement: With = independent (denominator stays same). Without = dependent (denominator shrinks).

5. Conditional Probability & Bayes' Theorem

Strategy — the 3-step Bayes recipe:
  1. Draw a tree (or write out the partition). First branches = causes/hypotheses. Second branches = evidence.
  2. Compute P(evidence) using total probability: $P(E) = \sum P(E|H_i) \cdot P(H_i)$
  3. Flip with Bayes: $P(H_i|E) = \frac{P(E|H_i) \cdot P(H_i)}{P(E)}$
Common trap: Confusing $P(B|A)$ with $P(A|B)$. Always ask: "What is my new world (denominator)?"

6. Naive Bayes Classification

Strategy:
  1. Compute prior for each class from training data.
  2. For each feature, compute likelihood $P(\text{feature}|\text{class})$. Use Laplace smoothing: $\frac{\text{count} + 1}{\text{total} + |V|}$
  3. Multiply: score = prior × product of all likelihoods.
  4. Higher score wins. No need to compute the denominator.
Common trap: Forgetting Laplace smoothing when a word has count 0 in a class.

7. Random Variable & Distribution Questions

Strategy:
  1. "Find k" → Set $\sum P(x) = 1$ (discrete) or $\int f(x)\,dx = 1$ (continuous), solve for $k$.
  2. E[X] = $\sum x \cdot P(x)$ or $\int x \cdot f(x)\,dx$.
  3. Var(X) = $E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$ (shortcut formula — always faster).
  4. P(a < X < b) = sum PMF values in range (discrete) or integrate PDF (continuous).
  5. Joint distributions: marginal = sum row/column. Independence check: does every cell = row total × column total?

8. Named Distribution Questions

Which distribution?
  • Fixed n trials, success/fail → Binomial $B(n,p)$
  • Rare events, large n, small p → Poisson $(\lambda = np)$
  • Bell-shaped, continuous → Normal $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$
  • "At least / at most" → Use complement or CDF
For Normal: always standardise first: $Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}$, then use Z-table.
Part 2: Formula Cheatsheet

Descriptive Statistics

$\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n}$    Median: middle value of sorted data    Mode: most frequent value
$s^2 = \frac{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1} = \frac{\sum x_i^2 - n\bar{x}^2}{n-1}$    $s = \sqrt{s^2}$    $\text{CV} = \frac{s}{\bar{x}} \times 100\%$
$\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1$    Fences: $Q_1 - 1.5 \cdot \text{IQR}$   to   $Q_3 + 1.5 \cdot \text{IQR}$
Pearson's $r = \frac{n\sum x_i y_i - \sum x_i \sum y_i}{\sqrt{[n\sum x_i^2 - (\sum x_i)^2][n\sum y_i^2 - (\sum y_i)^2]}}$
Skew: Mode $\approx$ 3 Median $-$ 2 Mean   |   Mean > Mode ⇒ right skew

Probability Rules

$P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$    $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$
$P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}$    $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B|A)$
Independent: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$   |   ME: $P(A \cap B) = 0$

Bayes' Theorem & Total Probability

$P(B) = \sum_{i} P(B|A_i) \cdot P(A_i)$    $P(A_i|B) = \frac{P(B|A_i) \cdot P(A_i)}{P(B)}$
Naive Bayes: $\hat{C} = \arg\max_{C_k} P(C_k) \prod_{i} P(x_i|C_k)$   Laplace: $P(w|C) = \frac{\text{count}(w,C) + 1}{\text{total}_C + |V|}$

Random Variables

$E[X] = \sum x \cdot P(x)$    $\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$    $\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$
CDF: $F(x) = P(X \le x)$   PDF: $P(a < X < b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$   Valid: $\sum P = 1$ or $\int f = 1$

Named Distributions

Binomial: $P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$   $\mu = np$, $\sigma^2 = np(1-p)$
Poisson: $P(X=k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}$   $\mu = \sigma^2 = \lambda$
Normal: $Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}$   $P(a < X < b) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{b-\mu}{\sigma}\right) - \Phi\!\left(\frac{a-\mu}{\sigma}\right)$
Normal approx to Binomial (when $np \geq 5$ and $n(1-p) \geq 5$): use $N(np, np(1-p))$ with continuity correction

Hypothesis Testing

Z-test: $Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}$    t-test: $t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}$, df $= n-1$
Paired t-test: $t = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d / \sqrt{n}}$ where $d_i = \text{after}_i - \text{before}_i$
Two-proportion: $Z = \frac{\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2}{\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}}$ where $\hat{p} = \frac{x_1+x_2}{n_1+n_2}$
F-test (variance): $F = \frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}$ (larger on top)   ANOVA: $F = \frac{MS_{\text{between}}}{MS_{\text{within}}}$

Regression & Correlation

$\hat{Y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X$   $\beta_1 = \frac{n\sum x_i y_i - \sum x_i \sum y_i}{n\sum x_i^2 - (\sum x_i)^2}$   $\beta_0 = \bar{y} - \beta_1 \bar{x}$

Exponential Smoothing

$F_{t+1} = \alpha \cdot A_t + (1 - \alpha) \cdot F_t$   ($\alpha$ close to 1 → reacts fast to changes)
Part 3: Quiz 1 — Full Solutions

Q1. Quantitative Variable

Which of the following is an example of a quantitative variable?
  • (a) The type of vehicle a person drives
  • (b) The age of a person
  • (c) The brand of a smartphone
  • (d) The colour of a shirt
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (b) The age of a person

Why: Quantitative = numerical, you can do maths on it. Age is measured in numbers (18, 34, 65). All others are categories (labels) — you can't average "SUV + Sedan".

Tip: Ask "Can I compute a meaningful average?" If yes → quantitative.

Q2. Discrete Random Variable

A discrete random variable is characterized by:
  • (a) Having an infinite number of possible values
  • (b) Taking only a countable set of values
  • (c) Being non-numeric
  • (d) Having no probability distribution
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (b) Taking only a countable set of values

Why: Discrete = countable (0, 1, 2, 3, ...). Even countably infinite (like "number of flips to get heads") is still discrete. Continuous = uncountable interval (height 172.345... cm).

Quick test: "Can I list the possibilities (even if the list is long)?" Yes → discrete.

Q3. NOT a Discrete Random Variable

Which is NOT an example of a discrete random variable?
  • (a) The number of pets owned by a family
  • (b) The number of students in a classroom
  • (c) The height of students in a classroom
  • (d) The number of books on a shelf
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (c) The height of students in a classroom

Why: Height is measured, not counted. It can take any value (165.5 cm, 165.51 cm, ...) — continuous. All others are counts of whole items.

Q4. Die + Coin Sample Space

When a die and a coin are rolled together, all possible outcomes are:
  • (a) 12
  • (b) 36
  • (c) 6
  • (d) 2
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (a) 12

Why: Fundamental Counting Principle: $6 \times 2 = 12$. Die has 6 outcomes, coin has 2, and they are independent. The sample space is {(1,H), (1,T), (2,H), ..., (6,T)}.

Q5. Conditional Probability — Card (Spade & Queen)

A card is drawn from a standard deck. Given the card is a spade, what is the probability it is a queen?
  • (a) 1/12
  • (b) 1/4
  • (c) 1/52
  • (d) 1/13
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (d) 1/13

Why: "Given it's a spade" shrinks the world to 13 spades. Among those 13, exactly 1 is the Queen of Spades. So $P(\text{queen}|\text{spade}) = \frac{1}{13}$.

Key idea: Conditioning = pick the right denominator (13, not 52).

Q6. Bayes' Theorem Purpose

Bayes' Theorem helps in:
  • (a) Predicting future stock market prices
  • (b) Finding maximum likelihood estimates
  • (c) Updating probabilities based on new evidence
  • (d) Solving linear algebra problems
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (c) Updating probabilities based on new evidence

Why: Bayes flips $P(\text{evidence}|\text{cause})$ into $P(\text{cause}|\text{evidence})$. It's a belief-updating machine. MLE is (b) — a different method. Stock prediction (a) and linear algebra (d) are unrelated.

Q7. Skewness from Mean & Mode

The distribution in which mean = 60 and mode = 55 will be:
  • (a) Negative skewed
  • (b) None
  • (c) Positive skewed
  • (d) Symmetrical
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (c) Positive skewed (right-skewed)

Why: Mean (60) > Mode (55). The mean is pulled toward the right tail. Rule: Mean > Mode ⇒ positively skewed.

Q8. "At Least One Graduate" — Complement + Combinations

5 of 20 men are graduates. If 3 are picked at random, the probability of at least one graduate is:
  • (a) 137/228
  • (b) 165/228
  • (c) 91/228
  • (d) 1/114
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (a) 137/228

Step-by-step:

  1. Total ways to pick 3 from 20: $\binom{20}{3} = \frac{20 \times 19 \times 18}{6} = 1140$
  2. "At least one grad" is easier via complement: $P = 1 - P(\text{no grads})$
  3. Ways to pick 3 non-grads (from 15): $\binom{15}{3} = \frac{15 \times 14 \times 13}{6} = 455$
  4. $P(\text{no grads}) = \frac{455}{1140} = \frac{91}{228}$
  5. $P(\text{at least 1}) = 1 - \frac{91}{228} = \frac{137}{228}$

Tip: Whenever you see "at least one", immediately think complement trick.

Q9. Grey Hair — Bayes' Theorem

5% of men and 0.25% of women have grey hair. Equal numbers of males and females. A grey-haired person is selected. P(male) = ?
  • (a) 8/11
  • (b) 20/21
  • (c) 13/21
  • (d) 10/11
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (b) 20/21

Step-by-step Bayes:

  1. $P(M) = P(W) = 0.5$, $P(G|M) = 0.05$, $P(G|W) = 0.0025$
  2. $P(G) = P(G|M) \cdot P(M) + P(G|W) \cdot P(W) = 0.05(0.5) + 0.0025(0.5) = 0.025 + 0.00125 = 0.02625$
  3. $P(M|G) = \frac{0.05 \times 0.5}{0.02625} = \frac{0.025}{0.02625} = \frac{25}{26.25} = \frac{500}{525} = \frac{20}{21}$

Insight: Men are 20× more likely to have grey hair, so grey hair is very strong evidence for male — despite equal priors.

Q10. Total Probability — First Class Marks

2/3 boys, 1/3 girls. P(first class | girl) = 0.25, P(first class | boy) = 0.28. P(first class) = ?
  • (a) 0.27
  • (b) 0.43
  • (c) 0.51
  • (d) 0.20
Answer & Explanation

Answer: (a) 0.27

Total probability:

$$P(\text{FC}) = P(\text{FC}|\text{Boy}) \cdot P(\text{Boy}) + P(\text{FC}|\text{Girl}) \cdot P(\text{Girl})$$ $$= 0.28 \times \frac{2}{3} + 0.25 \times \frac{1}{3} = 0.1867 + 0.0833 = 0.27$$

Q3. MCQ: Coefficient of Variation
Problem: Dataset A has mean 50, SD 10. Dataset B has mean 200, SD 30. Which has more relative variability?

Solution: $CV_A = \frac{10}{50} \times 100 = 20\%$, $CV_B = \frac{30}{200} \times 100 = 15\%$.
Dataset A has more relative variability despite having a smaller SD. CV normalises by the mean.
Q4. MCQ: Which measure is resistant to outliers?
Options: (a) Mean (b) Range (c) Median (d) Variance

Answer: (c) Median. The median only cares about position, not value. A billionaire walking into a room barely moves the median but drags the mean far right.

Q5. Independent Events — Full Workout (Exam EC2R Jul 2024)
P(A) = 0.35, P(B) = 0.30, independent. Validate and find:

(a) $P(A \cup B) = 0$ as they are independent? FALSE. Independence means $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B) = 0.105 \neq 0$. $P(A \cup B) = 0.35 + 0.30 - 0.105 = 0.545$.

(b) $P(A \cap B) = 0$ as they are mutually exclusive? FALSE. They are independent, not ME. $P(A \cap B) = 0.105$.

(c) $P(A^c \cap B^c) = P(\text{neither}) = 1 - P(A \cup B) = 1 - 0.545 = 0.455$. Or by independence: $(1-0.35)(1-0.30) = 0.65 \times 0.70 = 0.455$ ✓
Q6. Independent Events — All Operations (Exam EC2M Jul 2024)
P(A) = 0.25, P(B) = 0.20, independent.

(a) $P(A \cup B) = 0.25 + 0.20 - 0.05 = 0.40$
(b) $P(A \cap B) = 0.25 \times 0.20 = 0.05$
(c) $P(A/B) = P(A|B) = P(A) = 0.25$ (independent!)
(d) $P(B/A) = P(B|A) = P(B) = 0.20$ (independent!)
(e) $P(A^c/B) = P(A^c|B) = 1 - P(A|B) = 1 - 0.25 = 0.75$
(f) $P(A^c \cap B^c) = (1-0.25)(1-0.20) = 0.75 \times 0.80 = 0.60$
Q7. Three Events — Conditional Probabilities (Exam EC2R Jul 2024)
P(A)=0.50, P(B)=0.55, P(C)=0.45, P(A∩B)=0.20, P(B∩C)=0.20, P(A∩C)=0.15, P(A∩B∩C)=0.05.

First: $P(A \cup B) = 0.50 + 0.55 - 0.20 = 0.85$

(a) $P(A | A \cup B) = \frac{P(A \cap (A \cup B))}{P(A \cup B)} = \frac{P(A)}{P(A \cup B)} = \frac{0.50}{0.85} \approx 0.588$

(b) $P(B | A \cap B) = \frac{P(B \cap (A \cap B))}{P(A \cap B)} = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A \cap B)} = 1$

(c) $P(A \cap B | A \cup B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A \cup B)} = \frac{0.20}{0.85} \approx 0.235$

(d) $P(A \cup B | A \cap B \cap C) = \frac{P((A \cup B) \cap A \cap B \cap C)}{P(A \cap B \cap C)} = \frac{P(A \cap B \cap C)}{P(A \cap B \cap C)} = 1$ (since $A \cap B \cap C \subseteq A \cup B$)

Q10. Weather Alerts — Three Events (Exam EC2 Dec 2025)
A, B, C are weather alerts. Given: each equally likely, P(any two) same, P(all three), P(none).

Approach: Use inclusion-exclusion:
$P(A \cup B \cup C) = P(A)+P(B)+P(C) - P(AB) - P(BC) - P(CA) + P(ABC)$

Plug in the given values and compute step-by-step. The key is mastering inclusion-exclusion for 3 events.

Q13. Naive Bayes: "assignment urgent" (Exam EC2 Dec 2025)
Training data:
Urgent (2 msgs): "assignment due tomorrow please help", "exam rescheduled urgent attention" → words: assignment, due, tomorrow, please, help, exam, rescheduled, urgent, attention (9 words)
Not Urgent (2 msgs): "lunch plans today", "thanks for the notes" → words: lunch, plans, today, thanks, for, the, notes (7 words)

Priors: $P(U) = 2/4 = 0.5$, $P(NU) = 2/4 = 0.5$

Vocabulary $|V|$ = total unique words across all messages. Let's count: assignment, due, tomorrow, please, help, exam, rescheduled, urgent, attention, lunch, plans, today, thanks, for, the, notes = 16.

Classify "assignment urgent":
With Laplace ($\alpha = 1$):
$P(\text{assignment}|U) = \frac{1+1}{9+16} = \frac{2}{25}$, $P(\text{urgent}|U) = \frac{1+1}{9+16} = \frac{2}{25}$
$P(\text{assignment}|NU) = \frac{0+1}{7+16} = \frac{1}{23}$, $P(\text{urgent}|NU) = \frac{0+1}{7+16} = \frac{1}{23}$

$\text{Score}_U = 0.5 \times \frac{2}{25} \times \frac{2}{25} = 0.5 \times 0.0064 = 0.0032$
$\text{Score}_{NU} = 0.5 \times \frac{1}{23} \times \frac{1}{23} = 0.5 \times 0.00189 = 0.000945$

$0.0032 > 0.000945$ ⇒ Classified as Urgent

Q16. Find k, E[X], Var(X) for PMF (Exam EC2 Dec 2025)
x: 0, 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7   P(x): 0, k, 2k, 2k, 3k, k², 7k²+k

Find k: $\sum P = 0 + k + 2k + 2k + 3k + k^2 + 7k^2 + k = 9k + 8k^2 = 1$
$8k^2 + 9k - 1 = 0$. Using quadratic formula: $k = \frac{-9 + \sqrt{81 + 32}}{16} = \frac{-9 + \sqrt{113}}{16} \approx \frac{-9 + 10.63}{16} \approx 0.102$
Or: try $k = 0.1$: $8(0.01) + 9(0.1) = 0.08 + 0.9 = 0.98 \approx 1$. Close enough for exam — $k \approx 0.1$.

With k = 0.1: P(x): 0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.2, 0.3, 0.01, 0.17. Sum $\approx 0.98$ (approximate).

Q19. Joint Distribution — Independence Check (Exam EC2R Jul 2024)

(b) $P(X < 1, Y < 2) = P(X \in \{-1,0\}) \cdot P(Y \in \{0,1\}) = (0.25+0.15)(0.10+0.20) = 0.40 \times 0.30 = 0.12$

Q20. Joint PDF: f(x,y) = 6x²y on [0,1]×[0,1] (Exam EC2 Dec 2025)
Verify valid: $\int_0^1\int_0^1 6x^2 y\, dy\, dx = 6\int_0^1 x^2 dx \int_0^1 y\, dy = 6 \cdot \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 1$ ✓

Marginals:
$f_X(x) = \int_0^1 6x^2 y\, dy = 6x^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 3x^2$
$f_Y(y) = \int_0^1 6x^2 y\, dx = 6y \cdot \frac{1}{3} = 2y$

P(X<0.5, Y>0.5): $\int_0^{0.5}\int_{0.5}^1 6x^2 y\, dy\, dx = 6\int_0^{0.5} x^2 dx \int_{0.5}^1 y\, dy = 6 \cdot \frac{1}{24} \cdot \frac{3}{8} = 6 \cdot \frac{3}{192} = \frac{18}{192} = \frac{3}{32} = 0.09375$

Independent? $f(x,y) = 6x^2y = (3x^2)(2y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)$ ⇒ Yes, independent.

E[X] $= \int_0^1 x \cdot 3x^2\, dx = 3 \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}$
E[Y] $= \int_0^1 y \cdot 2y\, dy = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{2}{3}$
E[XY] $= E[X] \cdot E[Y] = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{2}$ (independent, so $E[XY] = E[X]E[Y]$)

Q29. Paired t-test: Revision Strategy (Exam EC3 Feb 2026)
Before: 32, 35, 30, 33, 31, 34   After: 36, 38, 34, 37, 35, 39
$d_i$ = After $-$ Before: 4, 3, 4, 4, 4, 5

$\bar{d} = \frac{24}{6} = 4$, $s_d = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(d_i - 4)^2}{5}} = \sqrt{\frac{0+1+0+0+0+1}{5}} = \sqrt{0.4} \approx 0.632$

$H_0: \mu_d = 0$ (no improvement), $H_1: \mu_d > 0$ (one-tailed)
$t = \frac{4}{0.632/\sqrt{6}} = \frac{4}{0.258} = 15.49$

$t_{0.05, 5} = 2.015$. Since $15.49 \gg 2.015$, reject $H_0$. The strategy significantly improves performance.

Q31. Two-Way ANOVA Setup (Exam EC3 Feb 2026)

Approach:
  1. $SS_{\text{Total}} = \sum(x_{ij} - \bar{x})^2$
  2. $SS_{\text{Rows}} = c \sum(\bar{x}_{i\cdot} - \bar{x})^2$ (irrigation methods, c=4 blocks)

Q38. MCQ: Which test for comparing means of two independent samples (unknown σ, small n)?
Answer: Independent two-sample t-test. Unknown $\sigma$ rules out Z-test. Independent samples (not paired). Small n means t-distribution, not normal approximation.
Q39. MCQ: In ANOVA, what does a large F-statistic indicate?
Answer: The variation between groups is much larger than variation within groups ⇒ the group means are likely different ⇒ reject $H_0$ of equal means.
Q40. MCQ: What is the 68-95-99.7 rule?
For a normal distribution:
• 68% of data falls within $\mu \pm 1\sigma$
• 95% within $\mu \pm 2\sigma$
• 99.7% within $\mu \pm 3\sigma$

Use case: Quick estimation without Z-tables. "Within 2 SD" ≈ 95%.

Q44. Sampling Distribution — Binomial Population (Exam EC2M Jul 2024)
Population: Binomial. Sample size n=45, $\bar{x}=10$, $s=2$.
Q: Does the sampling distribution of the mean follow binomial?

No. By the Central Limit Theorem (CLT), when $n \geq 30$, the sampling distribution of $\bar{X}$ is approximately Normal regardless of the population distribution. So $\bar{X} \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$ approximately.

For $n=25$ (part b): $n < 30$, so CLT may not apply well. The sampling distribution won't be perfectly normal.

For a Normal population with $n=9$ (part c): If the population is Normal, then $\bar{X}$ is exactly Normal for any $n$, even $n=9$.
