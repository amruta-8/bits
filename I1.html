⚡ One-Page Cheat Sheet

S1 · Descriptive statistics Mean \(\bar x=\frac{\sum x}{n}\); sample variance \(s^2=\frac{\sum(x-\bar x)^2}{n-1}\) (Bessel's \(n-1\)); SD \(=\sqrt{s^2}\).
Quartiles: \(Q_2\)=median; \(Q_1,Q_3\)=medians of lower/upper halves. IQR \(=Q_3-Q_1\).
Outlier fences: below \(Q_1-1.5\,\text{IQR}\) or above \(Q_3+1.5\,\text{IQR}\).
Skew: mean \(>\) median → right (positive); mean \(<\) median → left. CV \(=\frac{\sigma}{\mu}\times100\%\) (compare spread).
S2–S3 · Probability Addition: \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)\). Mutually exclusive: \(P(A\cap B)=0\). Independent: \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\).
Conditional: \(P(B|A)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(A)}\); multiplication \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B|A)\).
Total probability: \(P(B)=\sum_i P(A_i)P(B|A_i)\). At-least-one \(=1-P(\text{none})\).
S4 · Bayes & Naive Bayes \(\displaystyle P(H|E)=\frac{P(E|H)P(H)}{\sum_i P(E|H_i)P(H_i)}\).
Naive Bayes: pick class maximizing \(P(c)\prod_i P(x_i|c)\).
Laplace smoothing: \(P(w|c)=\dfrac{\text{count}(w,c)+1}{N_c+|V|}\) (\(|V|\)=vocab size). Avoids zero probabilities.
S5 · Random variables Valid: \(f(x)\ge0\), \(\sum f=1\) (or \(\int f=1\)). \(E[X]=\sum x f(x)\) or \(\int x f\,dx\); \(\text{Var}=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2\).
\(E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b\); \(\text{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\text{Var}(X)\).
Joint: marginal \(f_X(x)=\sum_y f(x,y)\); independent ⇔ \(f(x,y)=f_X(x)f_Y(y)\); \(F(x)=P(X\le x)\), \(f=F'\).
S6 · Distributions Binomial \(P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\), mean \(np\), var \(np(1-p)\).
Poisson \(P(X=k)=\dfrac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}\), mean=var=\(\lambda\); \(\lambda=np\) approximates Binomial (\(n\) large, \(p\) small).
Normal: \(z=\dfrac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\); inverse \(x=\mu+z\sigma\); \(\Phi(-z)=1-\Phi(z)\).
S7 · Sampling, CLT, CI CLT: \(\bar X\) is \(\sim\) Normal with mean \(\mu\), SE \(=\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\); \(z=\dfrac{\bar x-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\).
CI: \(\bar x\pm z_{\alpha/2}\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\) (\(\sigma\) known / \(n\ge30\)); use \(t\) if \(\sigma\) unknown & \(n<30\).
Sample size: \(n=\big(\dfrac{z\,\sigma}{E}\big)^2\) (round up).
15-second decision rules
"Find outliers"→ \(Q_1-1.5\,\text{IQR}\), \(Q_3+1.5\,\text{IQR}\)
mean ≫ median (one huge value)→ right-skewed; report the median as robust centre
"classify a text message"→ Naive Bayes + Laplace smoothing
"test positive, rare disease, P(disease)?"→ Bayes; expect a low posterior (base-rate)
counts of rare events / "per hour, per foot"→ Poisson, \(\lambda=\) rate × exposure
"\(n\) trials, success prob \(p\)"→ Binomial; if \(n\) large & \(p\) small → Poisson(\(np\))
"between two values, bell curve"→ Normal; \(z\)-scores then table
"sample mean of \(n\)…"→ CLT: divide \(\sigma\) by \(\sqrt n\) (SE)
find \(k\) / \(c\) in a PMF or PDF→ set total \(\sum f=1\) or \(\int f=1\), solve
mean = variance?→ Poisson diagnostic (if not equal, not Poisson)

🧭 Which Probability Distribution? — Decision Guide

Picking the right distribution is half the battle. First ask the single most important question: are you counting whole-number occurrences, or measuring a continuous quantity? Then follow the branch.

The decision tree (read top to bottom)

① COUNTING (results are \(0,1,2,\dots\)):

  • One single trial with two outcomes (success/failure)? → Bernoulli \((p)\).
  • A fixed number \(n\) of independent trials, each success-prob \(p\) constant, and you count successes? → Binomial \((n,p)\). Tell: "out of \(n\)", "in \(n\) trials", "% of \(n\) items".
  • No fixed \(n\) — events occur at an average rate over time / length / area, count is unbounded? → Poisson \((\lambda)\). Tell: "on average \(\lambda\) per hour/day/foot", "number of … per …".

② MEASURING (continuous quantity, bell-shaped): → Normal \((\mu,\sigma)\). Tell: "normally distributed", heights/weights/scores/lifetimes; "proportion above/below/between".

③ INFERENCE about a parameter (Session 7):

  • About a mean, with \(\sigma\) known or \(n\ge30\) → Z (standard Normal).
  • About a mean, with \(\sigma\) unknown and \(n<30\) → t (df \(=n-1\)).
  • About a variance, or goodness-of-fit / independence of categories → Chi-square \((\chi^2)\).
  • Comparing two variances / ANOVA → F.

⭐ Approximation shortcuts (very commonly examined) Binomial → Poisson when \(n\) is large and \(p\) is small (rule of thumb \(n\ge20\), \(p\le0.05\)); use \(\lambda=np\). Tell: "large batch", "tiny defect rate", "rare".
Binomial → Normal when \(np\ge5\) and \(n(1-p)\ge5\); use \(N(np,\,npq)\) with a continuity correction (\(\pm0.5\)). Tell: large \(n\), \(p\) not extreme, asked for a range like "between 84 and 95".
Poisson → Normal when \(\lambda\) is large (\(\ge10\)); use \(N(\lambda,\lambda)\).
⚠️ Fast disambiguators Binomial vs Poisson: is there a fixed number of trials \(n\) and a per-trial probability \(p\)? → Binomial. Only an average rate with no \(n\)? → Poisson.
Poisson check: if a question says "mean = variance" (or you compute them equal), it's Poisson; if mean ≠ variance, it can't be Poisson.
Binomial vs Normal: discrete success-counts = Binomial; a continuous "normally distributed" measurement = Normal.
Z vs t (for a mean): known \(\sigma\) or \(n\ge30\) → Z; unknown \(\sigma\) and \(n<30\) → t.

🧠 Question-Reading Hacks — turn words into math

Most ISM mistakes are translation mistakes, not calculation mistakes. These tables convert exam wording into the right symbols and the right method.

⭐ How to deduce \(P(A\mid B)\) ("probability of A given B") from a sentence

The 2-step trick Step 1 — find the CONDITION (that goes after the bar, = \(B\)): it's whatever is already known / assumed to have happened. Signal words: "given that", "if", "knowing that", "when", "among", "of the [group]", "for those who", "out of the … who". Everything after these words is \(B\) — it shrinks your universe to just that group (the denominator).
Step 2 — find the TARGET (that goes before the bar, = \(A\)): it's the thing whose chance you're asked for, measured within that group.
Then \(\;P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}\) — and with a table, simply \(\dfrac{\text{cell}(A\cap B)}{\text{total of group }B}\).

⚠️ The direction trap — \(P(A\mid B)\neq P(B\mid A)\) "The test is 99% accurate" almost always states a likelihood \(P(+\mid\text{disease})\) (sensitivity). The question "if you test positive, chance you have the disease" asks the reverse, \(P(\text{disease}\mid +)\). They are different numbers — when you're given the effect-given-cause but asked the cause-given-effect, that's your cue to use Bayes' theorem.

Which method? — Bayes vs Total Probability vs plain conditional

Decide in one read Plain conditional — you have a joint table or \(P(A\cap B)\) and \(P(B)\) directly → \(P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}\) (= cell ÷ group total).
Total probability — the world splits into mutually-exclusive causes \(A_1,\dots,A_k\) with known \(P(A_i)\) and \(P(B\mid A_i)\), and you want the overall \(P(B)\) → \(P(B)=\sum_i P(A_i)P(B\mid A_i)\). Tell: "X% come from source 1, Y% from source 2, …; defect/error/spam rate of each is …; overall probability?"
Bayes — same setup, but you're given the effect happened and asked which cause → \(P(A_i\mid B)=\frac{P(B\mid A_i)P(A_i)}{P(B)}\). Tell: "given it is defective/positive/promoted, probability it came from machine/source/group …".

Per-topic hacks & gotchas

Descriptive (S1) • Compute the two fences once: \(Q_1-1.5\,\text{IQR}\), \(Q_3+1.5\,\text{IQR}\).
• Mean \(>\) median ⇒ right-skew; the lone extreme value sits on the tail side.
• "Which average to report?" → if an outlier exists, say median (robust).
• Sample SD uses \(n-1\); population uses \(n\) — read which.
Probability (S2–S3) • "At least one" → \(1-P(\text{none})\), every time.
• Independent → multiply; mutually exclusive → \(P(A\cap B)=0\) (can't be both).
• "Without replacement" → probabilities change each draw.
Bayes / Naive Bayes (S4) • Rare condition + accurate test → posterior is surprisingly low (base rate).
• Numerator = one branch; denominator = sum of all branches.
• Any zero word-count ⇒ use Laplace smoothing or the whole product becomes 0.
• Compare class scores directly (no need to normalise); biggest wins.
Random variables (S5) • Find \(k\): set total \(=1\) (\(\sum p\) or \(\int f\)).
• \(\text{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\text{Var}(X)\) — the \(+b\) shift is irrelevant.
• Symmetric PDF ⇒ mean at the centre (no integral needed).
• Independent ⇒ \(E[XY]=E[X]E[Y]\) and \(f(x,y)=f_X f_Y\).
Distributions (S6) • mean = variance? → Poisson signature.
• Poisson \(\lambda\) scales with the interval (×3 hours ⇒ ×3 λ).
• Normal: \(P(X>\mu)=0.5\); "between" = subtract two \(\Phi\)'s; "top \(p\%\)" → inverse-\(z\) then \(x=\mu+z\sigma\).
• Large \(n\) + range question ⇒ Normal approx with continuity correction.
Sampling / CLT / CI (S7) • Sample mean ⇒ shrink \(\sigma\) to \(SE=\sigma/\sqrt n\) before the \(z\).
• \(\sigma\) unknown & \(n<30\) ⇒ use \(t\) (df \(n-1\)).
• Higher confidence ⇒ wider interval; bigger \(n\) ⇒ narrower.
• Sample size: \(n=(z\sigma/E)^2\), always round up.

SESSION 1

Descriptive Statistics

Central tendency

Mean \(\bar x=\frac{1}{n}\sum x_i\) (sensitive to outliers). Median = middle value when sorted (robust). Mode = most frequent value. Empirical relation for moderately skewed data: Mode \(\approx 3\,\text{Median}-2\,\text{Mean}\).

Variability (dispersion)

Range = max − min. Sample variance \(s^2=\dfrac{\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2}{n-1}\), SD \(s=\sqrt{s^2}\).
The divisor \(n-1\) (Bessel's correction) makes \(s^2\) an unbiased estimate of the population variance — using \(n\) would systematically under-estimate. (A "population" variance uses \(n\); read which the question wants.)
Coefficient of variation \(CV=\dfrac{s}{\bar x}\times100\%\) — lets you compare spread across datasets with different means (smaller CV = more consistent).

Five-number summary, quartiles & the box plot

Quartiles & IQR Sort the data. \(Q_2\) = median. \(Q_1\) = median of the lower half, \(Q_3\) = median of the upper half. IQR \(=Q_3-Q_1\) (spread of the middle 50%). Five-number summary = (Min, \(Q_1\), Median, \(Q_3\), Max).
⭐ 1.5×IQR outlier rule (asked every paper) A value is a potential outlier if it is below \(Q_1-1.5\,\text{IQR}\) or above \(Q_3+1.5\,\text{IQR}\). Compute both fences, then check each extreme value against them.

Note on quartile method: some papers use the "median-of-halves" method, others linear interpolation \(\big(\text{position}=p(n+1)\big)\). Both are accepted — state which you use. With an odd \(n\), exclude the median when forming the two halves.

Skewness — read it from mean vs median

Right (positive) skew: mean \(>\) median (a long right tail / a few large values pull the mean up). Left (negative) skew: mean \(<\) median. Symmetric: mean \(\approx\) median. A single huge value (e.g. 120 among values near 20) signals right skew + an outlier — then the median is the better summary of "typical".
Likely exam asks (Q1) "Compute mean, median, SD, IQR; identify outliers (1.5×IQR); comment on skewness" · "Give the five-number summary; is the process stable or is the extreme value a special cause?" · "Which measure (mean/median) should be reported and why?" · "Use CV to decide which machine is more consistent" · "Interpret a summary table (count/mean/SD/quartiles)".
SESSION 2

Basic Probability

Foundations

Sample space \(S\) = all outcomes; an event is a subset. Axioms: \(0\le P(A)\le1\), \(P(S)=1\), and for mutually exclusive events \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)\).

Combining events

Key rules General addition: \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)\).
Mutually exclusive (disjoint): \(P(A\cap B)=0\) → can't both happen.
Independent: \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B)\) → one doesn't affect the other.
Complement: \(P(A^c)=1-P(A)\); at least one \(=1-P(\text{none})\).
Inclusion–exclusion (3 sets): \(P(A\cup B\cup C)=\sum P-\sum P(\text{pairs})+P(A\cap B\cap C)\).
⚠️ Classic trap "Independent" and "mutually exclusive" are not the same — in fact two events with non-zero probability that are mutually exclusive can never be independent (if disjoint, knowing one happened forces the other to be impossible). Papers love asking you to validate/refute such statements.

Counting (when outcomes are equally likely)

\(P(\text{event})=\dfrac{\#\text{favourable}}{\#\text{total}}\). Use permutations \(^nP_r=\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}\) when order matters, combinations \(\binom{n}{r}=\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\) when it doesn't (e.g. committee selection, card hands).

Likely exam asks (Q2/Q6) "Given \(P(A),P(B),P(A\cap B)\) find \(P(A\cup B)\) / exactly one / neither" (Venn) · "Validate: 'because independent, \(P(A\cup B)=0\)'" · "Fill the missing probabilities and check consistency" · "Inclusion–exclusion for defects" · "Combinatorial probability (committee, dice)".
SESSION 3

Conditional & Total Probability

Conditional probability & the multiplication rule

\(\displaystyle P(B\mid A)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(A)}\quad\Rightarrow\quad P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B\mid A).\)
Independence test: \(A,B\) independent ⇔ \(P(B\mid A)=P(B)\) ⇔ \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\).

With vs without replacement: drawing with replacement keeps trials independent (probabilities unchanged); without replacement makes them dependent (the pool shrinks) — use the multiplication rule with updated counts.

Law of total probability

Partition the sample space If \(A_1,\dots,A_k\) partition \(S\) (mutually exclusive, exhaustive), then for any event \(B\): $$P(B)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}P(A_i)\,P(B\mid A_i).$$ This is the "weighted average" of the conditional probabilities — the workhorse for spam/error/defect/channel problems and the denominator of Bayes' theorem.

Tree-diagram tip: each branch multiplies along its path; sum the paths that produce the target event. Contingency tables: \(P(B|A)=\dfrac{\text{cell}}{\text{row/col total}}\).

Likely exam asks (Q3/Q6) "Total probability that a message is spam / an item is defective" · "From a contingency table, find \(P(\text{no default}\mid\text{Middle income})\)" · "Two draws without replacement: \(P(\text{2nd red}\mid\text{1st blue})\)" · "Are 'from airport A' and 'late' independent?" · "Multi-event conditional with at-least conditions".
SESSION 4

Bayes' Theorem & Naive Bayes

Bayes' theorem

Posterior from prior × likelihood $$P(H\mid E)=\frac{P(E\mid H)\,P(H)}{P(E)}=\frac{P(E\mid H)\,P(H)}{\sum_i P(E\mid H_i)\,P(H_i)}.$$ The denominator is just total probability. Recipe: (1) write priors \(P(H_i)\); (2) write likelihoods \(P(E\mid H_i)\); (3) total probability \(P(E)\); (4) divide. Posteriors over all hypotheses sum to 1 — a built-in check.
Base-rate effect (the headline result) For a rare condition, even a "99% accurate" test gives a low posterior when positive, because the many healthy people generate lots of false positives. E.g. disease 0.1%, sensitivity 99%, specificity 98% → \(P(\text{disease}\mid+)\approx4.7\%\). Always expect a small number and explain why.

Naive Bayes classification

To classify an item with features \(x_1,\dots,x_m\), pick the class \(c\) that maximises the (proportional) posterior — assuming features are conditionally independent given the class:

$$\hat c=\arg\max_c\ P(c)\prod_{i=1}^{m}P(x_i\mid c).$$ Compare the class scores \(P(c)\prod P(x_i|c)\) (no need to normalise). The bigger score wins.
⭐ Laplace (add-one) smoothing — needed almost every paper A word never seen in a class would give probability 0 and wipe out the whole product. Fix it: $$P(w\mid c)=\frac{\text{count}(w,c)+1}{N_c+|V|}$$ where \(N_c\) = total word count in class \(c\) and \(|V|\) = vocabulary size (distinct words across all classes). Add 1 to every count, add \(|V|\) to every denominator.

Text NB recipe: (1) priors \(P(c)=\frac{\#\text{docs in }c}{\#\text{docs}}\); (2) build vocabulary, count words per class; (3) Laplace-smoothed \(P(w|c)\); (4) score each class \(=P(c)\prod_w P(w|c)\) over the new message's words; (5) argmax. Show the two scores and conclude.

Likely exam asks (Q2) "Classify this new message (spam/ham, urgent/not) with Naive Bayes + Laplace smoothing — show the scores" · "Given priors & likelihoods, find \(P(\text{cause}\mid\text{effect})\)" (VC/research, machine/defect, plant/quality) · "Disease test: \(P(\text{disease}\mid +)\); why so low?" · "Naive Bayes on a categorical table (weather→play, promotion→purchase)".
SESSION 5

Random Variables & Distributions

Discrete random variables (PMF)

Validity, expectation, variance A PMF \(p(x)\) needs \(p(x)\ge0\) and \(\sum_x p(x)=1\) (this is how you find an unknown \(k\)).
\(E[X]=\sum_x x\,p(x)\); \(E[X^2]=\sum_x x^2 p(x)\); \(\text{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2\); SD \(=\sqrt{\text{Var}}\).
Linear rules: \(E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b\); \(\text{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\text{Var}(X)\) (the shift \(b\) does not change variance — e.g. \(\text{Var}(2X+3)=4\,\text{Var}(X)\)).

Continuous random variables (PDF & CDF)

A PDF \(f(x)\) needs \(f(x)\ge0\) and \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx=1\) (find \(k\) here too). Probabilities are areas: \(P(a<X<b)=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\).
\(E[X]=\int x f(x)\,dx\), \(E[X^2]=\int x^2 f(x)\,dx\). CDF \(F(x)=P(X\le x)=\int_{-\infty}^{x} f\); conversely \(f(x)=F'(x)\). For a symmetric PDF about \(m\), \(E[X]=m\) by symmetry.

Joint distributions

Marginal, conditional, independence Find the constant from \(\sum_{x,y} f=1\) (discrete) or \(\iint f=1\) (continuous).
Marginals: \(f_X(x)=\sum_y f(x,y)\) (or \(\int f\,dy\)); similarly \(f_Y(y)\).
Conditional: \(f(y\mid x)=\dfrac{f(x,y)}{f_X(x)}\).
Independent ⇔ \(f(x,y)=f_X(x)\,f_Y(y)\) for all \(x,y\). If independent, \(E[XY]=E[X]E[Y]\).

Worked sketch: \(f(x,y)=6x^2y\) on \([0,1]^2\) ⇒ \(f_X=3x^2,\ f_Y=2y\); since \(3x^2\cdot2y=6x^2y\), independent; \(E[X]=\int_0^1 3x^3dx=\tfrac34\), \(E[Y]=\tfrac23\), \(E[XY]=\tfrac12\).

Likely exam asks (Q3a/Q4) "Find \(k\); compute \(P(X<6)\), \(E[X]\), Var, \(E[2X-3]\)" · "Continuous PDF: find \(k\), \(P(1<X<3)\), \(E[X]\)" · "Joint PDF: verify valid, marginals, independence, \(E[XY]\)" · "CDF given → find \(k\) and the PDF" · "Hypergeometric: draw without replacement, distribution of #defectives".
SESSION 6

Probability Distributions

Bernoulli & Binomial

Bernoulli = single trial, success prob \(p\). Binomial \(X\sim B(n,p)\) = number of successes in \(n\) independent trials: $$P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k},\quad E[X]=np,\quad \text{Var}=np(1-p).$$ (From mean \(np\) and variance \(npq\) you can back out \(n,p,q\).)

Poisson

For counts of rare events over a fixed interval/exposure: \(X\sim\text{Poisson}(\lambda)\), $$P(X=k)=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^{k}}{k!},\qquad E[X]=\text{Var}(X)=\lambda.$$ \(\lambda=\) rate × exposure (e.g. 1.2 visits/hr over 3 hrs ⇒ \(\lambda=3.6\)). Mean = variance is the Poisson diagnostic (if they differ, it isn't Poisson). Poisson approximates Binomial when \(n\) is large and \(p\) small, with \(\lambda=np\).

Common moves: \(P(X\ge1)=1-P(0)=1-e^{-\lambda}\); \(P(X\ge2)=1-e^{-\lambda}(1+\lambda)\); add exposures by adding \(\lambda\).

Normal distribution

Standardise, then use the Z-table \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\). Z-score \(z=\dfrac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\); then \(P(X<x)=\Phi(z)\) from the table.
\(P(a<X<b)=\Phi(z_b)-\Phi(z_a)\); left tail \(\Phi(-z)=1-\Phi(z)\).
Inverse normal (cut-offs): \(x=\mu+z\sigma\) where \(z\) is the value with the required tail area (top 10% → \(z=1.28\), top 5% → \(1.645\)).

Normal approximation to Binomial: for large \(n\), \(B(n,p)\approx N(np,\,np(1-p))\); apply a continuity correction (\(P(X\le k)\approx\Phi(\frac{k+0.5-\mu}{\sigma})\)). The \(t\), \(\chi^2\), \(F\) distributions are introduced here too (used in S7 / hypothesis testing): \(t\) for small samples with unknown \(\sigma\); \(\chi^2\) for variances / goodness-of-fit; \(F\) for ratios of variances.

Likely exam asks (Q3b/Q5) "Poisson: \(P(\text{no defect})\), \(P(\text{exactly one})\), packets out of 10,000" · "Normal: proportion of washers/resistors out of spec; comment on process capability" · "Inverse normal: Dean's-list cut-off / top 10%" · "Binomial exact vs Poisson approximation — compare" · "Binomial mean & SD → find \(n,p\), then a probability" · "Normal approx to Binomial with continuity correction".
SESSION 7

Sampling, Central Limit Theorem & Confidence Intervals

Sampling distribution of the mean & the CLT

The key idea If you draw samples of size \(n\), the sample mean \(\bar X\) has mean \(\mu\) and standard error $$SE=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}.$$ The Central Limit Theorem: for large \(n\) (rule of thumb \(n\ge30\)), \(\bar X\) is approximately Normal regardless of the population shape; if the population is already Normal, \(\bar X\) is exactly Normal for any \(n\). To find probabilities about \(\bar X\): \(z=\dfrac{\bar x-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\), then the Z-table.

Note: \(SE\) shrinks like \(1/\sqrt n\) — quadrupling \(n\) halves the spread of \(\bar X\). That's why larger samples give more precise estimates.

Estimation & confidence intervals

CI for a mean $$\bar x\ \pm\ z_{\alpha/2}\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\quad(\sigma\text{ known or }n\ge30),\qquad \bar x\ \pm\ t_{\alpha/2,\,n-1}\,\frac{s}{\sqrt n}\quad(\sigma\text{ unknown},\ n<30).$$ \(z_{\alpha/2}\): 90% → 1.645, 95% → 1.96, 99% → 2.576. The margin of error is \(E=z_{\alpha/2}\,\sigma/\sqrt n\).
Required sample size To achieve margin of error \(E\): \(\;n=\Big(\dfrac{z_{\alpha/2}\,\sigma}{E}\Big)^2\) — always round up to the next integer.
Which interval/test? quick guide \(\sigma\) known (or \(n\ge30\)) → use \(z\). \(\sigma\) unknown & \(n<30\) → use \(t\) (df \(=n-1\)). Higher confidence (99% vs 95%) → wider interval. Larger \(n\) → narrower interval. (Full hypothesis-test procedures, ANOVA and regression are post-mid-sem; the mid-sem stays at sampling distributions, CLT and confidence intervals.)

P1. Independent events — validate statements — 2023 Regular, Q2 · 4M
Question (verbatim) \(A\) and \(B\) are independent events, \(P(A)=0.35\), \(P(B)=0.30\). Validate and justify: (a) \(P(A\cup B)=0\) because independent; (b) \(P(A\cap B)=0\) because mutually exclusive; (c) Find \(P(A^c\cap B^c)\). [4]
Solution

Independence ⇒ \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)=0.35\times0.30=0.105\).

(a) Incorrect. \(P(A\cup B)=0.35+0.30-0.105=\mathbf{0.545}\), not 0. (Independence has nothing to do with the union being 0.)

(b) Incorrect. Mutually exclusive would need \(P(A\cap B)=0\), but here \(P(A\cap B)=0.105\neq0\). Independent ≠ mutually exclusive.

(c) \(A^c,B^c\) are also independent: \(P(A^c\cap B^c)=P(A^c)P(B^c)=0.65\times0.70=\mathbf{0.455}\). (Check: \(=1-P(A\cup B)=1-0.545=0.455\) ✓.)

P4. Three-event conditional probabilities — 2023 Regular, Q3 · 4M
Question (verbatim) Events with \(P(A)=0.50, P(B)=0.55, P(C)=0.45, P(A\cap B)=0.20, P(B\cap C)=0.20, P(A\cap C)=0.15, P(A\cap B\cap C)=0.05\). Find (a) \(P(A\mid A\cup B)\), (b) \(P(B\mid A\cap B)\), (c) \(P(A\cap B\mid A\cup B)\), (d) \(P(A\cup B\mid A\cap B\cap C)\). [4]

Solution

First verify: \(\int_0^2\!\int_0^1\frac{x+y}{3}\,dy\,dx=\frac13\int_0^2(x+\tfrac12)dx=\frac13(2+1)=1\) ✓.

(ii) \(f_X(x)=\int_0^1\frac{x+y}{3}dy=\frac{1}{3}\big(x+\tfrac12\big)=\dfrac{2x+1}{6}\) for \(0<x<2\).

(iii) \(f_Y(y)=\int_0^2\frac{x+y}{3}dx=\frac{1}{3}(2+2y)=\dfrac{2(1+y)}{3}\) for \(0<y<1\).

(iv) \(f_X(x)f_Y(y)\neq f(x,y)\) (a product of a function of \(x\) and a function of \(y\) can't equal \(x+y\)) ⇒ not independent.

(i) \(P(X>Y)=\displaystyle\int_0^1\!\!\int_y^2\frac{x+y}{3}\,dx\,dy\) (since \(x\) ranges up to 2 and \(y<1\le x\) on most of the region) \(=\frac13\int_0^1\big[\tfrac{x^2}{2}+yx\big]_{y}^{2}dy=\frac13\int_0^1\big(2+2y-\tfrac{3y^2}{2}\big)dy=\frac13\big(2+1-\tfrac12\big)=\mathbf{5/6}\).

\(P(M_2\mid D)=\dfrac{100}{260}\approx\mathbf{0.385}\).

A6. Valid distribution, \(E[X]\), variance, \(P(X\ge3)\) — Asgn 1, Sec 1, Q3 · 5M · S5
Question (verbatim) FreshMart customers \(X\): 0,1,2,3,4 with \(P(X)\): 0.10, 0.25, 0.35, 0.20, 0.10. (1) Verify it is a valid distribution. (2) Expected number of customers. (3) Variance. (4) \(P(\text{at least 3})\).
Solution

(1) All \(\ge0\) and \(0.10+0.25+0.35+0.20+0.10=1\) ✓ valid.

(2) \(E[X]=0(0.1)+1(0.25)+2(0.35)+3(0.2)+4(0.1)=\mathbf{1.95}\).

(3) \(E[X^2]=0+0.25+1.40+1.80+1.60=5.05\); \(\text{Var}=5.05-1.95^2=\mathbf{1.2475}\).

(4) \(P(X\ge3)=0.20+0.10=\mathbf{0.30}\).

A7. Descriptive stats with an outlier (which centre to report) — Past-yr Asgn 1, Sec 1, Q1 · S1
Question (verbatim) Monthly maintenance costs (thousands): 18, 20, 19, 21, 23, 20, 22, 19, 18, 20, 21, 120. (a) mean, median, mode; left- or right-skewed? (b) five-point summary and IQR. (c) which measure (mean/median) should be used for decision-making, and why?
Solution

Sort: 18,18,19,19,20,20,20,21,21,22,23,120 (n=12). (a) Sum \(=341\) ⇒ mean \(\approx28.4\); median \(=(20+20)/2=20\); mode \(=20\). Mean ≫ median ⇒ right-skewed (the 120 drags the mean up).

(b) \(Q_1=\) median of first six \(=(19+19)/2=19\); \(Q_3=\) median of last six \(=(21+22)/2=21.5\); summary \(=(18,19,20,21.5,120)\); IQR \(=2.5\). Upper fence \(=21.5+3.75=25.25\) ⇒ 120 is an outlier.

(c) Use the median (20): it is robust to the 120 outlier, whereas the mean (28.4) misrepresents a typical month.

A8. Naive Bayes from a categorical table (subscribe by device) — Past-yr Asgn 1, Sec 2, Q3 · S4
Question (verbatim) Newsletter "Subscribe" by Device Type: V1 Mobile→No, V2 Mobile→Yes, V3 Desktop→Yes, V4 Desktop→No, V5 Tablet→Yes. (i) priors \(P(\text{Yes}),P(\text{No})\); (ii) a new Mobile visitor — Naive Bayes prediction of Subscribe.
Solution

(i) Yes: V2,V3,V5 → \(P(\text{Yes})=3/5\); No: V1,V4 → \(P(\text{No})=2/5\).

(ii) Likelihoods of Device=Mobile: among Yes, only V2 is Mobile → \(P(\text{Mobile}|\text{Yes})=1/3\); among No, only V1 is Mobile → \(P(\text{Mobile}|\text{No})=1/2\). Scores: Yes \(=\tfrac35\cdot\tfrac13=0.20\); No \(=\tfrac25\cdot\tfrac12=0.20\). Tie — with Laplace smoothing (3 device types, \(|V|=3\)): \(P(\text{Mobile}|\text{Yes})=\tfrac{1+1}{3+3}=\tfrac13\), \(P(\text{Mobile}|\text{No})=\tfrac{1+1}{2+3}=\tfrac25\); Yes \(=0.6\cdot0.333=0.20\), No \(=0.4\cdot0.40=0.16\) ⇒ predict Subscribe = Yes.

A9. Normal distribution — crop yield (counts & top 10%) — Past-yr Asgn 2, Sec 2, P2 · S6
Question (verbatim) Yield per plot is Normal \((\mu=662\) kg\(,\sigma=32\) kg\()\), 1000 plots. (a) number of plots yielding more than 700 kg; (b) number yielding less than 650 kg; (c) the minimum yield of the top 10% of plots.
Solution

(a) \(z=\dfrac{700-662}{32}=1.19\); \(P(X>700)=1-\Phi(1.19)=1-0.8830=0.1170\) → \(\approx\mathbf{117}\) plots.

(b) \(z=\dfrac{650-662}{32}=-0.375\); \(P(X<650)=\Phi(-0.375)\approx0.354\) → \(\approx\mathbf{354}\) plots.

(c) Top 10% ⇒ \(z=1.28\); cut-off \(=662+1.28(32)=662+40.96\approx\mathbf{703}\) kg.

A10. Standard error & confidence interval (carpet strength) — Past-yr Asgn 2, Sec 7/8 · S7
Question (verbatim) Carpet tuft bind strength: population mean target 50 kg, \(\sigma=10\) kg, sample \(n=40\), sample mean \(\bar x=52\) kg. (a) standard error of the sample mean; (b) 95% confidence interval; (c) effect of using 98% confidence; (e) sample size for a margin of error of 1 kg at 95%.
Solution

(a) \(SE=\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}=\dfrac{10}{\sqrt{40}}=\mathbf{1.58}\) kg.

(b) 95% (\(z=1.96\)): \(52\pm1.96(1.58)=52\pm3.10=\mathbf{(48.9,\ 55.1)}\) kg.

(c) 98% (\(z=2.326\)) widens it: \(52\pm2.326(1.58)=52\pm3.68=(48.3,55.7)\) — higher confidence ⇒ wider interval (less precision).

(e) \(n=\big(\dfrac{1.96\times10}{1}\big)^2=384.16\Rightarrow n=\mathbf{385}\) (round up).

W1. Coefficient of variation — which machine is more consistent? — practice · S1
Problem Machine A: mean 50 mm, SD 2 mm. Machine B: mean 100 mm, SD 3 mm. Which is more consistent?
Solution

Compare relative spread: \(CV_A=\tfrac{2}{50}\times100\%=4\%\); \(CV_B=\tfrac{3}{100}\times100\%=3\%\). \(3\%<4\%\) ⇒ Machine B is more consistent (lower CV). (SD alone would mislead — B's SD is larger but its mean is much larger too.)

W2. Mutually exclusive vs independent — \(P(A\cup B)\) — practice · S2
Problem \(P(A)=0.3,\ P(B)=0.4\). Find \(P(A\cup B)\) if (a) mutually exclusive, (b) independent.
Solution

(a) ME ⇒ \(P(A\cap B)=0\): \(P(A\cup B)=0.3+0.4=\mathbf{0.7}\). (b) Independent ⇒ \(P(A\cap B)=0.3\times0.4=0.12\): \(P(A\cup B)=0.3+0.4-0.12=\mathbf{0.58}\). Different assumptions, different answers.

W3. Total probability — noisy channel — companion S3
Problem A channel sends 1 with prob 0.4 (0 with 0.6). If 0 is sent it is received correctly 90% of the time; if 1 is sent, 95%. Find \(P(\text{a 1 is received})\).
Solution

A received-1 comes from (sent 1, correct) or (sent 0, flipped): \(P(\text{rec }1)=0.4(0.95)+0.6(0.10)=0.38+0.06=\mathbf{0.44}\).

W4. Bayes — three coins — practice · S4
Problem Three coins: \(C_1\) fair \((P(H)=0.5)\), \(C_2\) two-headed \((1.0)\), \(C_3\) biased \((0.75)\). Pick one at random and flip → Heads. \(P(\text{it was }C_2)\)?
Solution

Priors \(1/3\) each. \(P(H)=\tfrac13(0.5+1.0+0.75)=0.75\). \(P(C_2\mid H)=\dfrac{1.0\times\tfrac13}{0.75}=\dfrac{1/3}{3/4}=\mathbf{4/9\approx0.44}\).

W5. Continuous PDF — find \(c\), \(P(X>1)\) — practice · S5
Problem \(f(x)=c(4x-2x^2)\) for \(0<x<2\). Find \(c\) and \(P(X>1)\).
Solution

\(\int_0^2 c(4x-2x^2)dx=c[2x^2-\tfrac{2x^3}{3}]_0^2=c(8-\tfrac{16}{3})=\tfrac{8c}{3}=1\Rightarrow c=\mathbf{3/8}\). \(P(X>1)=\tfrac38\int_1^2(4x-2x^2)dx=\tfrac38[2x^2-\tfrac{2x^3}{3}]_1^2=\tfrac38(\tfrac83-\tfrac43)=\tfrac38\cdot\tfrac43=\mathbf{0.5}\).

W6. Joint PMF — \(c\), marginal, conditional — practice · S5
Problem \(P(x,y)=c(x+y)\) for \(x=1,2;\ y=1,2\). Find \(c\), the marginal of \(X\), and \(P(Y=1\mid X=2)\).
Solution

Cells: \(2c,3c,3c,4c\Rightarrow12c=1\Rightarrow c=\mathbf{1/12}\). Marginal \(P(X{=}1)=5c=5/12,\ P(X{=}2)=7c=7/12\). \(P(Y{=}1\mid X{=}2)=\dfrac{P(2,1)}{P(X{=}2)}=\dfrac{3/12}{7/12}=\mathbf{3/7}\).

W7. Poisson via complement; Normal between bounds — practice · S6
Problem (a) \(X\sim\text{Poisson}(4)\): find \(P(X\ge2)\). (b) Salaries \(N(\mu=50\text{k},\sigma=10\text{k})\): \(P(45\text{k}<X<60\text{k})\)?
Solution

(a) \(P(0)=e^{-4}=0.0183,\ P(1)=4e^{-4}=0.0733\); \(P(X\ge2)=1-0.0916=\mathbf{0.9084}\).

(b) \(z_1=\dfrac{45-50}{10}=-0.5,\ z_2=\dfrac{60-50}{10}=1\); \(P=\Phi(1)-\Phi(-0.5)=0.8413-0.3085=\mathbf{0.5328}\).

W8. Confidence interval & two-sided z reasoning — practice · S7
Problem \(n=100,\ \bar x=50,\ s=5\). Give the 95% CI for the mean and state how it changes at 99%.
Solution

\(SE=5/10=0.5\). 95% (\(z=1.96\)): \(50\pm0.98=[49.02,50.98]\). At 99% (\(z=2.576\)): \(50\pm1.288=[48.71,51.29]\) — wider, because higher confidence demands a larger margin.

🧮 Master Formula Sheet

Descriptive (S1) \(\bar x=\frac{\sum x}{n}\); \(s^2=\frac{\sum(x-\bar x)^2}{n-1}\); IQR \(=Q_3-Q_1\); outlier fences \(Q_1-1.5\,\text{IQR}\), \(Q_3+1.5\,\text{IQR}\); CV \(=\frac{s}{\bar x}\times100\%\); skew from mean vs median.
Probability (S2–S3) \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)\); independent \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\); \(P(B|A)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(A)}\); total probability \(P(B)=\sum P(A_i)P(B|A_i)\); at-least-one \(=1-P(\text{none})\).
Bayes / Naive Bayes (S4) \(P(H|E)=\frac{P(E|H)P(H)}{\sum_i P(E|H_i)P(H_i)}\); NB \(\hat c=\arg\max_c P(c)\prod_i P(x_i|c)\); Laplace \(P(w|c)=\frac{\text{count}+1}{N_c+|V|}\).
Random variables (S5) \(\sum f=1\) or \(\int f=1\); \(E[X]=\sum x f\) / \(\int xf\); \(\text{Var}=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2\); \(E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b\), \(\text{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\text{Var}(X)\); independent ⇔ \(f(x,y)=f_X f_Y\); \(F(x)=P(X\le x),\ f=F'\).
Distributions (S6) Binomial \(\binom{n}{k}p^k q^{n-k}\), mean \(np\), var \(npq\); Poisson \(\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}\), mean=var=\(\lambda\), \(\lambda=np\); Normal \(z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\), inverse \(x=\mu+z\sigma\), \(\Phi(-z)=1-\Phi(z)\).
Sampling / CLT / CI (S7) \(SE=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\); \(z=\frac{\bar x-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\); CI \(=\bar x\pm z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\) (\(t\) if \(\sigma\) unknown, \(n<30\)); sample size \(n=\big(\frac{z\sigma}{E}\big)^2\) (round up). \(z\): 90%→1.645, 95%→1.96, 99%→2.576.

🎯 Last-Minute Exam-Hall Card

  1. Descriptive: sort first; \(Q_2\) then split halves for \(Q_1,Q_3\); fences \(\pm1.5\,\text{IQR}\); mean ≫ median ⇒ right-skew, report median.
  2. "Find \(k\)/\(c\)": set \(\sum p=1\) (discrete) or \(\int f=1\) (continuous), solve.
  3. \(E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b\), \(\text{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\text{Var}(X)\) — the shift never changes variance.
  4. Bayes: priors × likelihoods on top, total probability on bottom; rare-disease positives give a low posterior — say why (base rate).
  5. Naive Bayes: build vocabulary, use Laplace \(\frac{\text{count}+1}{N_c+|V|}\), compare class scores \(P(c)\prod P(x_i|c)\), argmax. Show both scores.
  6. Poisson: \(\lambda=\) rate × exposure; add exposures by adding \(\lambda\); \(P(\ge1)=1-e^{-\lambda}\). Use it to approximate Binomial when \(n\) big, \(p\) small.
  7. Normal: standardise \(z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\), read \(\Phi\); "between" \(=\Phi(z_2)-\Phi(z_1)\); inverse \(x=\mu+z\sigma\) (top 10% → 1.28, top 5% → 1.645).
  8. CLT/sample mean: divide \(\sigma\) by \(\sqrt n\) (SE) before computing \(z\).
  9. CI: \(\bar x\pm z\,\sigma/\sqrt n\); use \(t\) (df \(n-1\)) when \(\sigma\) unknown & \(n<30\); higher confidence → wider; sample size \(n=(z\sigma/E)^2\), round up.
  10. Independence checks: \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\)? Mutually exclusive ≠ independent.
  11. Sanity-check: probabilities in \([0,1]\), variance \(\ge0\), Bayes posteriors sum to 1, proportions × N ≈ counts.
  12. Always write the formula, then substitute — method earns most of the marks even if a Z-table read is slightly off.
